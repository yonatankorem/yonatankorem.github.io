## On the craft

*I've spent fourteen years building systems that couldn't afford to be wrong. That leaves a mark on how you think.*

Avionics. Medical imaging. Insurance. The domains shifted; the constraint didn't. When errors have real consequences — a missed diagnosis, a failed regulatory decision, a split-second system fault — quality stops being a preference and becomes a load-bearing wall. That's the environment that shaped me, and it's the standard I carry into every team I work with.

I think of myself as a **pragmatic craftsman**. Quality and professionalism drive me. Pragmatism and business orientation are what I learned, over time, are equally essential — because the best architecture in the world is worthless if it doesn't ship, doesn't scale with the business, or can't be understood by the team maintaining it.

## What I bring to a team

The most meaningful thing I can do for a team isn't deliver a technical artefact — it's raise the ceiling of what they believe is possible, then build the conditions where they don't need me to hold the wheel.

That means setting standards others can own, making architectural decisions legible across the team, and creating genuine autonomy for engineers who have all the skills needed to move independently. The best teams I've been part of feel less like a hierarchy and more like a group of craftsmen who trust each other completely.

I've always worked as a true full-stack engineer — backend, frontend, infrastructure, CI/CD, Terraform, design, grooming. That breadth isn't a résumé point; it's what lets me draw accurate system boundaries and have honest conversations across every layer of a product.

On **agentic AI in engineering workflows**: this is the most important shift in how software gets built today, and I'm deeply embedded in it — not as an observer, but as someone actively shaping how teams adopt it. The tools change. The underlying standards of quality and ownership don't.

That's not so different from good software architecture.
